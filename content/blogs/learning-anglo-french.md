### Overview

Learning Anglo-French: French Language-Learning Manuscripts in Britain, c.1200–c.1500 is an ERC/UKRI-funded research project based at the University of Exeter. Learning Anglo-French (LAF) will operate over a five-year period (2023-28), and will offer the first sustained response to the question of how the French language was learned in Britain between the 13th and 16th centuries.

### Context

![Learning Anglo-French manuscript](blogs-img/laf_manuscript.jpg)

Learning Anglo-French manuscript

French in medieval Britain was both a language of culture and power and a second mother tongue for a bilingual minority of the population. This unusual situation makes it a key case study for grasping how multilingual societies work, yet the nature and methods of its transmission remain mysterious. ‘Learning Anglo-French’ (LAF) combines traditional and cutting-edge methodologies to address vital questions about the history of vernacular language acquisition and multilingualism in medieval Britain. Its central corpus is a group of over fifty manuscripts made in Britain between the 13th and 15th centuries containing texts used to teach French, the earliest known tradition of modern language pedagogy.

### Technologies and Processes

As part of the project team, I am the developer on Learning Anglo-French, contributing to the creation of an eXist-db native XML database and web interface for manuscripts and their constituent texts. My work includes designing and implementing the database architecture and interface, as well as developing the XQuery functionality, XSLT transformations and XML schemas.

I am exploring ways to bring together textual data, images, metadata, biocodicological information and bibliography within a manuscript-centred data model. The LAF resource is designed to present multiple interconnected views of each manuscript, allowing users to navigate between different layers of information and linked records. I am modelling these relationships in eXist-db and developing functionality that supports cross-referencing, extended search capabilities and different forms of presentation. This allows users to explore individual texts and manuscripts in close detail, while also tracing wider connections through a clear and intuitive interface.

Drawing on Digital Humanities methods and best practices, the project uses the internationally recognised Text Encoding Initiative (TEI) Guidelines to create standardised and machine-readable descriptions of its materials. By connecting the TEI encoded texts, database and user interface, I am helping to build an open-access research resource that presents the project’s findings in interactive, searchable and reusable formats. The TEI XML files will also be deposited and published in a public code repository, supporting long-term preservation, interoperability and reusability.

Alongside the database and editions, I am also developing a mapping tool using GIS technologies to visualise the origins and provenance of specific texts and manuscripts. This tool will offer new ways of exploring how these materials were produced, transmitted, and studied across medieval Britain.
